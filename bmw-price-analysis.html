Exploring Factors that Influence Used BMW Prices¶

Main Research Question:¶

What factors are most strongly associated with the price of used BMW vehicles?

Problem Definition¶

The goal of this project is to explore what factors are most strongly associated with the price of used BMW vehicles. Used car pricing is influenced by many characteristics including age, mileage, engine size, transmission type, and fuel type. Understanding how these factors relate to price can provide insight into depreciation patterns.

This question is relevant to car buyers, sellers, dealerships, and automotive analysts. Buyers may want to understand what features justify higher prices, while sellers may want to understand how their vehicle compares to others on the market.

Rather than attempting to predict price, this project focuses on identifying patterns and relationships using exploratory data analysis.

Data Description¶

The dataset used in this project comes from Kaggle (BMW Cars Dataset) and contains listings of used BMW vehicles. Each row represents one vehicle listing. The dataset contains 10,781 rows and 9 columns.

The columns include:

  • model: BMW model name
  • year: Year the vehicle was manufactured
  • price: Listing price
  • transmission: Transmission type
  • mileage: Total miles driven
  • fuelType: Fuel type (petrol, diesel, hybrid)
  • tax: Road tax amount
  • mpg: Miles per gallon
  • engineSize: Engine size in liters

The dataset does not include information about vehicle condition, accident history, or geographic location. These missing factors may influence price but are not captured in this dataset. Therefore, conclusions from this dataset are limited to the variables provided.

Visualization Summary¶

To understand which factors are most strongly associated with price, I examined several visualizations along with a correlation matrix.

The correlation matrix reveals that the strongest relationships with price are year, car_age, and mileage. This suggests that newer vehicles and vehicles with lower mileage tend to have higher prices.

The negative correlation between car_age and price indicates clear depreciation over time. Similarly, higher mileage is strongly associated with lower prices, reinforcing the impact of vehicle usage on value.

Engine size shows a moderate positive correlation with price (0.46), while other variables, such as mpg and tax, show weaker relationships with price.

Storytelling & Interpretation¶

The data suggests that vehicle age and mileage are the strongest factors associated with used BMW prices. Both variables show strong negative relationships with price, reflecting expected depreciation patterns in the automotive resale market.

Newer vehicles command higher prices, and vehicles with lower mileage are generally more expensive. These findings align with basic economic intuition, as buyers typically value vehicles that are newer.

Engine size also shows a moderate positive association with price, suggesting that higher performance/ luxury models may command higher prices. However, the relationship is clearly not as strong as age or mileage. This indicates that there are other factors influencing price outside of engine size.

It is also always important to note that correlation does not imply causation. While age and mileage are strongly associated with price, this analysis does not prove they directly cause price changes. Other factors, not included in the dataset, such as vehicle condition, accident history, and geographic location very likely play a significant role.

Overall, the data tells a consistent story of depreciation influenced primarily by age and mileage.

Limitations, Ethics, & Reflection¶

Although the dataset provides valuable insight, it has several limitations as well.

First, the dataset only includes BMW vehicles, so findings cannot be generalized to all car brands. Additionally, the dataset does NOT include information about vehicle condition, accident history, geographic location, or seller type (private or dealer). These factors likely influence price but are not captured in this dataset.

The dataset also reflects listing prices rather than final sales prices, which could introduce bias if sellers consistently overprice or underprice vehicles.

There may also be sampling bias, if the dataset reflects listings from a specific market or country.

If additional data were available, I would explore regional price variation and comparison across different car brands.

This project reinforced that data analysis requires careful interpretation and awareness of what the data does and does not capture.
