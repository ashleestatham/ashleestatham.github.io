Why Learning Data Science in Social and Interdisciplinary Contexts Matters

Published: January 2026 | 4 min read

Learning data science in social and interdisciplinary contexts matters because data is not just numbers — it represents people, behaviors, and real-world outcomes.

Modeling social topics allows us to understand patterns that are often invisible at the individual level, such as inequality and access to resources. When used correctly, data science can positively impact public policy, healthcare, education, and technology.

At the same time, working with data about people comes with responsibilities. Data is rarely neutral, and how it is collected and interpreted can create bias. Protecting privacy, avoiding harmful assumptions, and acknowledging limitations are essential responsibilities.

Interdisciplinary learning matters because social problems require more than technical skills. Perspectives from sociology, psychology, ethics, and public policy are critical to responsible analysis.

Through DTSC Studio II, I aim to strengthen both technical and critical thinking skills so I can use data science to contribute to ethical and informed decision-making.

Social Determinants of Health and the Capabilities Approach

Published: February 2026 | 5 min read

When thinking about health, the first thing I picture is hospitals, doctors, and medications. However, most of what shapes our health happens long before we enter a clinic.

During Data Science Studio II, I learned about the Social Determinants of Health (SDOH), defined as the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age. I now see SDOH as everyday circumstances that quietly structure our chances of living a healthy life.

Housing, employment, neighborhood safety, education, and social inclusion all shape health outcomes. A child in damp housing may develop asthma. Someone with stable employment is more likely to afford nutritious food and experience lower stress levels.

The Capabilities Approach shifts focus from what people have to what they are able to be and do. It asks whether people truly have the freedom to live the life they value.

Comparing SDOH and the Capabilities Approach shows that social conditions shape people’s capabilities. Unsafe neighborhoods, poor housing, and discrimination limit not only health outcomes, but also freedom, opportunity, and participation in society. SDOH form the foundation on which capabilities either grow or are constrained.
